Introduction

        Seismic waves are generated at a source, which can be natural, such as an earthquake or artificial, such as an explosion. The generated waves travel through the earth or in other words, through the medium and are recorded at a receiver. The receiver is known as the seismometer and the record of the ground motion at the seismometer is called the seismogram. A seismogram, contains information about both the source and the medium.

The generated seismic waves provide information on the location and the nature of the source. If the origin time when the waves left the source is known, their arrival time at the receiver gives the travel time required to pass through the medium. This time gives information about the speed at which they traveled and generally about the physical properties of the medium. In addition because the amplitude of the waves are affected by the medium, the signals observed on seismograms provide additional information about the portion of the earth that the waves traveled through.

Properties of the seismic waves

        Several properties of the seismic waves are analogous to familiar concepts in the propagation of light and sound. One of these properties is the refraction. Seismic wave velocities generally increase with depth. Because of the different velocities at different depths, wave paths bend away from the vertical as they go deeper into the earth, eventually become horizontal, turn upward, and return to the surface. The wave paths are thus used to infer the variation of seismic velocity, and hence the composition and physical properties of material, with depth in the earth. Another property is the reflection that occurs when a seismic wave travels to an interface, across which physical properties change, such as the boundary between the earth's mantle and core. In addition to refraction and reflection, waves also undergo diffraction where seismic waves bend around obstacles such as the earth's core.

Earthquakes

        The location of an earthquake is known as the focus or hypocenter and it can be found from the arrival times of seismic waves recorded on seismometers at different sites. This location is often shown by the epicenter, the point on the earth's surface above the earthquake. The size of earthquakes is measured from the amplitude of the motion recorded on seismograms and given in terms of magnitude or moment.

Seismic phases

        Seismograms are records of the motion of the ground resulting from the arrival of refracted, reflected and diffracted seismic waves. The recorded ground motion is a vector and thus three different components are typically recorded (north-south, east-west, up-down). The generated seismic waves consist of different type of waves. The body waves consist of the P or compressional waves that material moves back and forth in the direction in which the wave propagates and the S or shear waves that the material moves at right angles to the propagation direction. P waves are faster that S waves thus the first arriving pulse is a direct P wave. Different other phases of compressional waves can occur after the direct P waves, for example when the wave reflects the surface and then is recorded at the receiver (PP), when then wave reflects the outer core and then end up at the receiver (PcP) or when the wave travels through the mantle, the outer and the inner core and then end up at the surface (PKIKP). Among the later arrivals on the seismogram are shear wave phases including the direct arrival S and a shear phase SS that reflected off the surface, analogous to PP. The large amplitude wave train that arrives later signifies the arrival of different waves called surface waves. Rayleigh wave is an example of this wave. Such waves propagate along paths close to the earth's surface.
